## Executive Summary

This analysis evaluates **24 major ethical philosophers** spanning 2,400 years (Aristotle to Peter Singer) to determine which thinkers' approaches align with:
- **Rules-Based Systems (287 Rules Model):** Comprehensive, specific, enforceable codes
- **Principle-Based Systems (PIE 2.0 Framework):** Minimalist, adaptable, judgment-requiring principles

**Key Findings:**
- **Medieval/Religious philosophers** (Aquinas, Augustine) favor rules-based systems (divine law, natural law codes)
- **Enlightenment rationalists** (Kant, Spinoza) favor principle-based systems (single universal principles)
- **Utilitarian tradition** (Bentham, Mill, Singer) straddles both (one principle generates many rules)
- **Virtue ethicists** (Aristotle, MacIntyre) align with PIE 2.0 (character-based, not rule-based)
- **Contemporary care ethicists** (Gilligan, Noddings) strongly align with PIE 2.0 (relational, context-sensitive)

**Verdict:** **13 philosophers align with PIE 2.0**, **6 align with 287 Rules**, **5 are hybrid/neutral**

## 1. Introduction: Evaluation Criteria {#introduction}

### 1.1 The Evaluation Framework

Each philosopher is assessed on **six dimensions**:

| Dimension | Rules-Based (287) | Principle-Based (PIE 2.0) |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| **1. Rule-Generativity** | HIGH (many specific rules) | LOW (few abstract principles) |
| **2. Specificity** | Concrete, detailed directives | Abstract, requires interpretation |
| **3. Adaptability** | LOW (fixed to context) | HIGH (applies across contexts) |
| **4. Agency Emphasis** | LOW (external compliance) | HIGH (internal judgment) |
| **5. Cultural Portability** | LOW (culturally-embedded) | HIGH (universal principles) |
| **6. Complexity** | HIGH (exhaustive coverage) | LOW (elegant simplicity) |

### 1.2 Scoring System

Each philosopher receives a score from -3 to +3:
- **+3:** Strongly aligns with PIE 2.0 (principle-based)
- **0:** Neutral or hybrid
- **-3:** Strongly aligns with 287 Rules (rules-based)

### 1.3 The 24 Philosophers

Selected based on:
- Historical significance in ethical theory
- Diversity of approaches (virtue, deontology, consequentialism, care ethics)
- Chronological coverage (ancient to contemporary)

## 2. The Philosophers: Chronological Survey {#philosophers}

### 2.1 Ancient Period (384 BCE - 430 CE) {#ancient}

---

#### **1. Aristotle (384-322 BCE)**

**Core Theory:** Virtue ethics (Nicomachean Ethics)

**Key Concepts:**
- 12 moral virtues (mean between vices)
- Phronesis (practical wisdom) required to determine the mean
- Eudaimonia (flourishing) as ultimate good
- Character cultivation through habituation

**Rule-Generativity:** MEDIUM
- 17 virtues total (12 moral + 5 intellectual)
- Each virtue requires judgment in application
- No codified rules, only virtues to cultivate

**Alignment Analysis:**

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Rule-Generativity | +2 | Only 17 virtues (not hundreds of rules) |
| Specificity | +2 | Abstract virtues, not concrete directives |
| Adaptability | +3 | Phronesis applies across all contexts |
| Agency Emphasis | +3 | Requires practical wisdom (not rule-following) |
| Cultural Portability | +1 | Some virtues are culturally specific (magnificence) |
| Complexity | +2 | Simpler than comprehensive rules |

**Total Score: +13 / 18 = +2.17 (Strongly PIE 2.0)**

**Verdict:** **ALIGNS WITH PIE 2.0**
- Virtue ethics emphasizes character and judgment over rules
- Phronesis (practical wisdom) = "Know Thyself" + "Serve Growth"
- Mean between extremes = context-sensitive application

---

#### **2. Epicurus (341-270 BCE)**

**Core Theory:** Hedonistic ethics (pleasure as highest good)

**Key Concepts:**
- Ataraxia (tranquility) and aponia (freedom from pain)
- Prudent calculation of pleasures
- Simple living, friendship, withdrawal from politics
- Tetrapharmakos (four-part cure for anxiety)

**Rule-Generativity:** LOW
- Few specific principles
- Main rule: "Seek pleasure, avoid pain" (but requires calculation)

**Alignment Analysis:**

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Rule-Generativity | +3 | Minimal principles |
| Specificity | +1 | "Seek pleasure" is somewhat vague |
| Adaptability | +2 | Applies across contexts but needs calculation |
| Agency Emphasis | +2 | Requires prudent judgment |
| Cultural Portability | +2 | Universal (all seek pleasure) |
| Complexity | +3 | Very simple |

**Total Score: +13 / 18 = +2.17 (Strongly PIE 2.0)**

**Verdict:** **ALIGNS WITH PIE 2.0**
- Minimalist principle-based approach
- Emphasizes individual judgment and calculation

---

#### **3. Stoics (Zeno, Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius) (300 BCE - 180 CE)**

**Core Theory:** Stoic ethics (virtue is sufficient for happiness)

**Key Concepts:**
- Live according to nature (Logos)
- Four cardinal virtues: wisdom, courage, justice, temperance
- Dichotomy of control (focus on what you can control)
- Amor fati (love of fate)

**Rule-Generativity:** LOW
- 4 cardinal virtues
- Few specific rules, emphasis on inner attitude

**Alignment Analysis:**

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Rule-Generativity | +3 | Only 4 virtues |
| Specificity | +2 | Abstract virtues |
| Adaptability | +3 | Universal principles |
| Agency Emphasis | +3 | Extreme emphasis on internal judgment |
| Cultural Portability | +3 | Universal Logos |
| Complexity | +3 | Extremely simple |

**Total Score: +17 / 18 = +2.83 (Extremely PIE 2.0)**

**Verdict:** **STRONGLY ALIGNS WITH PIE 2.0**
- 4 virtues = same number as PIE 2.0's 4 principles!
- Emphasis on wisdom, self-reflection, autonomy
- "Know Thyself" echoes Stoic self-examination

---

#### **4. St. Augustine (354-430 CE)**

**Core Theory:** Christian ethics (love of God as supreme good)

**Key Concepts:**
- Love God above all, love neighbor as self
- Original sin and need for divine grace
- Two cities: City of God vs. City of Man
- Divine command theory elements

**Rule-Generativity:** MEDIUM-HIGH
- Two great commandments (love God, love neighbor)
- But interpreted through extensive Church law
- 10 Commandments as foundation

**Alignment Analysis:**

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Rule-Generativity | 0 | 2 great commandments, but 10 Commandments + Church law |
| Specificity | -1 | Mix of abstract (love) and concrete (Commandments) |
| Adaptability | -1 | Tied to Christian theology |
| Agency Emphasis | -2 | Obedience to divine will (not autonomous judgment) |
| Cultural Portability | -2 | Christian-specific |
| Complexity | 0 | Simple principles, complex theology |

**Total Score: -6 / 18 = -1.0 (Leans toward Rules)**

**Verdict:** **HYBRID (Leans toward 287 Rules)**
- Two great commandments are principle-based
- But foundation in divine law and commandments is rules-based
- Church tradition generates extensive moral rules

### 2.2 Medieval Period (1033 - 1349) {#medieval}

---

#### **5. St. Anselm (1033-1109)**

**Core Theory:** Christian ethics with philosophical rigor

**Key Concepts:**
- Ontological argument for God
- Ethics grounded in divine nature
- Justice as rightness of will

**Rule-Generativity:** MEDIUM-HIGH
- Grounded in divine commandments
- Natural law tradition

**Alignment:** **-1.5 (Leans toward Rules)** - Divine law emphasis

---

#### **6. St. Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274)**

**Core Theory:** Natural law ethics (Summa Theologica)

**Key Concepts:**
- Natural law derived from eternal law
- Four types of law: eternal, natural, human, divine
- Synderesis (natural inclination to good)
- Five primary precepts of natural law

**Rule-Generativity:** HIGH
- 5 primary precepts (preserve life, reproduce, educate, live in society, worship God)
- Generates extensive secondary precepts
- Casuistry (case-based moral reasoning)

**Alignment Analysis:**

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Rule-Generativity | -2 | 5 precepts generate many secondary rules |
| Specificity | -2 | Detailed casuistry for specific cases |
| Adaptability | -1 | Tied to natural law metaphysics |
| Agency Emphasis | 0 | Mix of reason (agency) and obedience (law) |
| Cultural Portability | -1 | Christian natural law framework |
| Complexity | -2 | Extensive scholastic system |

**Total Score: -8 / 18 = -1.33 (Rules-Based)**

**Verdict:** **ALIGNS WITH 287 RULES**
- Natural law generates extensive moral rules
- Casuistry provides specific guidance for cases
- Systematic, comprehensive moral theology

---

#### **7. William of Ockham (1287-1347)**

**Core Theory:** Nominalism and divine command theory

**Key Concepts:**
- Ockham's Razor (simplicity principle)
- Divine command theory (good = what God commands)
- Nominalism (universals don't exist independently)

**Rule-Generativity:** HIGH (if God commands many things), but philosophically minimalist

**Alignment:** **+1.0 (Slightly PIE 2.0)** - Ockham's Razor favors minimalism

### 2.3 Early Modern Period (1469 - 1724) {#early-modern}

---

#### **8. Niccolò Machiavelli (1469-1527)**

**Core Theory:** Political realism (The Prince)

**Key Concepts:**
- Ends justify means (for political stability)
- Virtù (political skill, not moral virtue)
- Fortune vs. skill
- Consequentialist amorality

**Rule-Generativity:** MEDIUM
- Pragmatic maxims, not moral rules
- Context-dependent political advice

**Alignment:** **0 (Neutral)** - Not a moral philosopher per se

- State of nature = war of all against all
- Social contract to escape state of nature
- Sovereign power enforces peace
- Laws of nature = rational precepts for peace

**Rule-Generativity:** MEDIUM-HIGH

- Extensive legal framework required for civil society

**Alignment Analysis:**

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |

| Adaptability | 0 | Universal human nature, but specific institutions |
| Agency Emphasis | -2 | Obedience to sovereign |
| Cultural Portability | +1 | Universal human psychology |
| Complexity | -1 | Systematic but complex |

**Total Score: -4 / 18 = -0.67 (Leans toward Rules)**

**Verdict:** **HYBRID (Leans toward 287 Rules)**
- Many laws of nature
- Emphasis on external enforcement (sovereign)
- Less emphasis on internal agency

**Core Theory:** Rationalist ethics (Ethics)

**Key Concepts:**
- God = Nature (pantheism)
- Freedom through understanding necessity
- Conatus (striving to persevere in being)
- Intellectual love of God

**Rule-Generativity:** LOW
- Single principle: understand and accept necessity
- Geometric method (few axioms generate all conclusions)

**Alignment Analysis:**

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Rule-Generativity | +3 | Few axioms, geometric deduction |
| Specificity | +1 | Abstract metaphysical principles |
| Adaptability | +2 | Universal (applies to all beings) |

| Complexity | +1 | Simple foundations, complex derivations |

**Total Score: +13 / 18 = +2.17 (Strongly PIE 2.0)**

**Verdict:** **ALIGNS WITH PIE 2.0**
- Minimalist axiomatic approach
- Emphasis on understanding (Know Thyself)
- Freedom through wisdom, not rule-following

#### **11. John Locke (1632-1704)**

**Core Theory:** Natural rights theory (Two Treatises of Government)

**Key Concepts:**
- Natural rights: life, liberty, property
- State of nature with natural law
- Social contract to protect rights
- Separation of powers

**Rule-Generativity:** MEDIUM
- 3 natural rights
- Natural law generates specific rights and duties

**Alignment:** **+0.5 (Slightly PIE 2.0)** - Few core rights, but generates legal frameworks

### 2.4 Enlightenment (1711 - 1804) {#enlightenment}

---

#### **12. David Hume (1711-1776)**

**Core Theory:** Sentimentalism (Treatise of Human Nature, Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals)

**Key Concepts:**
- Morality based on sentiment, not reason
- Is/ought problem (cannot derive ought from is)
- Utility as basis for moral approval
- Natural virtues and artificial virtues

**Rule-Generativity:** LOW
- No systematic moral rules
- Emphasis on sentiment and convention
- Proto-utilitarianism (utility generates approval)

**Alignment Analysis:**

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Rule-Generativity | +2 | Few general principles |
| Specificity | +2 | Abstract (sentiment, utility) |
| Adaptability | +3 | Applies across cultures (universal sentiments) |
| Agency Emphasis | +2 | Moral sense requires judgment |
| Cultural Portability | +2 | Universal human sentiments |
| Complexity | +2 | Relatively simple |

**Total Score: +13 / 18 = +2.17 (Strongly PIE 2.0)**

**Verdict:** **ALIGNS WITH PIE 2.0**
- Minimalist, sentiment-based approach
- Emphasis on judgment and feeling (not rules)
- Proto-utilitarian (one principle: utility)

---

#### **13. Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712-1778)**

**Core Theory:** General will and natural goodness (The Social Contract, Emile)

**Key Concepts:**
- Humans naturally good, corrupted by society
- General will (collective good) vs. will of all
- Social contract based on general will
- Education cultivates natural virtue

**Rule-Generativity:** LOW
- Single principle: follow the general will
- Emphasis on cultivating natural sentiments

**Alignment:** **+1.5 (PIE 2.0)** - Principle-based, emphasizes cultivation

---

- Autonomy and rationality
- Humanity as end-in-itself
- Kingdom of ends

**Rule-Generativity:** VERY LOW

| Cultural Portability | +3 | Rationality is universal |
| Complexity | +3 | One principle, elegant |

**Total Score: +18 / 18 = +3.0 (PERFECT PIE 2.0)**

**Verdict:** **MOST STRONGLY ALIGNS WITH PIE 2.0**
